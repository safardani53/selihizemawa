George Clarke transforms rundown Victorian house with a hazardous 70s MDF staircase into a fam

GEORGE Clarke transformed a 19th century cottage with a ‘hazardous’ 1970s staircase into a modern family home.

Vicky and Paul were the latest couple to appear on Channel Four’s Old House New Home, after living in their ‘disjointed’ pad for nine years.

For nearly a decade the couple, who have three children, had planned to renovate their 1850s home, in Lingfield, Surrey, but were overwhelmed by the task.

Vicky said: “It’s a bit disjointed isn’t it. The kitchen is separate from the dining room.

“It doesn’t really work for us, but we don’t know how to make it work for us.”

Describing the mash up in the home, Paul said: “From a 70s MDF staircase to a 1850s wall that’s now inside.”

While the house may be Victorian, it suffered a 1970s revamp which left it with a bizarre layout, a ‘super outdated’ kitchen, a faux gothic fireplace, a corridor which led to nowhere – and an ‘aggressive’ staircase.

Butting out into the middle of the hallway, the stairway looks as if it’s been put in backwards, with George branding it just “wrong”’.

With a hazardous overhang which blocked the natural light, George added it was quirky to say the least.

He said: “It’s how to make not a bad shape feel so much smaller by putting the staircase in kind of the wrong way really.

“That’s ridiculous.”

As he sets out a plan to modernise the home, George says: “It’s the hall that needs the biggest re-think.

“We’ve got to turn the staircase round. It’s mad.

“The current staircase doubles back on itself, not only creating a hazardous overhang but completely blocks the window too.”

The team get to work to transform the cottage into a modern family home, by adding doors in the corridor to nowhere, scrapping the fireplace and knocking through a wall to make an open plan kitchen diner.

But the biggest change is in the entrance hall, where the ‘aggressive’ staircase finally gets turned round the right way.

Revealing their new home - which previously had some ‘seriously dodgy design features’ – George shows off their grand entrance hall.

Simply turning the stairs round made “all the difference”, George said.

Paul added: “From something which used to dominate, you almost don’t see it.

“When it first went in, I was almost disappointed. You were so excited about a new staircase, than it’s like ‘oh’.

“But that’s the whole point; it doesn’t get in the way anymore.”
